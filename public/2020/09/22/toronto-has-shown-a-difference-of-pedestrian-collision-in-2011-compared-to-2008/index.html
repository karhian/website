Toronto has shown a difference of pedestrian collision in 2011 compared to 2008

2020-09-22

Kar Hian Ong

Abstract

Pedestrian is part and parcel of life in a city. In this paper, we explore the transportation data for the years 2008 and 2011 which is an observational dataset. We find that there exists some correlation between pedestrian collision and TTC stops. Our findings have implications for forecasting pedestrian collision prone areas and the factors that may cause it.

Introduction

With health being the talk of the world, pedestrian safety is equally important. There are instances of pedestrians being killed\((^6)\) in the streets of Toronto. Being a pedestrian myself I wonder how safe is the streets of Toronto. I also wanted to find out if there is any correlation with other variables so that the city can make changes to make the street safer for pedestrians.

With 140 neighbourhoods, I selected neighbourhoods with pedestrian collision numbers greater than 700 to plot a bar chart. This is a concern because it shows that there are on average more than 2 incidences a day in those neighbourhoods.

Data

I have obtained a dataset from Open Data Toronto (Gelfand (2020)). This dataset consists of transportation data from 140 neighbourhoods in Toronto for the year 2008 and 2011. Below is an overview of the 2008 dataset. The data set is broken into 2 sheets one for 2008 and the other 2011. 2011 datasets contain the same variables as in 2008.
